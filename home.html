Atopic Dermatitis (Eczema) Management Tips

  • Please consult a healthcare professional for a personalized treatment plan.
  • If symptoms worsen or become infected, seek medical advice.
  • Consult a Doctor: If you suspect eczema, see a healthcare professional for diagnosis and a treatment plan. If it's severe or you experience difficulty breathing, swelling, or signs of infection, call 911.
  • Moisturize: Regularly apply a fragrance-free, hypoallergenic moisturizer, especially after bathing, to keep your skin hydrated.
  • Avoid Triggers: Identify and avoid triggers such as specific soaps, detergents, fabrics, or foods that worsen your eczema.
  • Gentle Cleansing: Use mild, fragrance-free cleansers and lukewarm water. Pat dry gently with a soft towel.
  • Cotton Clothing: Wear loose, cotton clothing. Avoid wool and synthetics that can irritate your skin.
  • Short, Lukewarm Showers: Use lukewarm water for short showers and gentle cleansers to avoid skin irritation.
  • Prescription Medications: For severe cases, doctors may prescribe topical corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, or other anti-inflammatory treatments.
  • Antihistamines: Over-the-counter or prescription antihistamines can help reduce itching, especially at night.
  • Wet Wrap Therapy: In severe cases, your doctor may recommend wet wrap therapy to reduce inflammation and lock in moisture.
  • Allergen Management: Identify and avoid allergens that trigger your eczema. Allergy testing may help.
  • Stress Management: Manage stress through techniques like deep breathing, yoga, or meditation, as stress can worsen symptoms.
  • Diet: Monitor your diet for potential triggers. Keeping a food diary and consulting a healthcare professional can help.

Contact Dermatitis Management Tips

  • Please consult a healthcare professional for a personalized treatment plan.
  • If you're unsure about the condition or if it's severe, please consult a healthcare professional.
  • In case of a severe allergic reaction or difficulty breathing, call 911 or seek immediate medical attention.
  • Identify and Avoid the Trigger: Determine the substance causing the rash and avoid further contact with it. This might include certain chemicals, plants, metals, or cosmetics.
  • Cleanse the Area: Gently wash the affected area with mild soap and lukewarm water to remove any remaining irritants. Pat the skin dry with a soft towel.
  • Topical Steroids: Over-the-counter hydrocortisone creams may help relieve itching and inflammation. Follow the product's instructions or consult a doctor for a stronger prescription if needed.
  • Cold Compress: Apply a cold, damp compress to the affected area for 15-20 minutes to reduce itching and swelling.
  • Moisturize: Keep the area well-hydrated by applying a hypoallergenic, fragrance-free moisturizer to lock in moisture. This can also help prevent further irritation.
  • Antihistamines: Over-the-counter antihistamines, such as diphenhydramine (Benadryl), can alleviate itching and discomfort.
  • Avoid Scratching: Try to resist scratching, as it can worsen the rash and lead to infection. Keep your nails short and wear gloves at night if needed.
  • Prescription Medications: In severe cases, a healthcare provider may prescribe stronger topical steroids, oral steroids, or other medications to manage symptoms.
  • Barrier Creams: If you're exposed to irritants in your daily activities, consider using protective barrier creams to prevent contact with potential triggers.
  • Allergen Testing: For chronic or recurrent cases, consider allergy testing to identify specific allergens that may be causing the contact dermatitis.
  • Avoid Harsh Soaps and Detergents: Use mild, fragrance-free cleansers and detergents to reduce the risk of skin irritation.
  • Clothing: Wear loose-fitting, breathable clothing made of natural fibers like cotton, and avoid tight synthetic fabrics.

Urticaria (Hives) Management Tips

  • Please consult a healthcare professional for a personalized treatment plan.
  • If you're unsure about the condition or if it's severe, please consult a healthcare professional.
  • In the case of a severe allergic reaction, difficulty breathing, swelling of the face or throat, or other severe symptoms, call 911 or seek immediate medical attention.
  • Identify and Avoid Triggers: Try to determine the cause of your hives and avoid any known allergens or triggers. Common triggers can include certain foods, medications, insect stings, and allergens.
  • Over-the-Counter Antihistamines: Non-prescription antihistamines, such as cetirizine (Zyrtec) or loratadine (Claritin), can help relieve itching and reduce hives. Follow the recommended dosing instructions.
  • Prescription Medication: In severe cases or when over-the-counter antihistamines are not effective, your healthcare provider may prescribe stronger antihistamines, corticosteroids, or other medications to manage symptoms.
  • Cool Compresses: Applying cold, damp compresses to the affected areas can help soothe itching and reduce inflammation.
  • Avoid Hot Water: When bathing or showering, use lukewarm water rather than hot water, which can exacerbate hives.
  • Loose Clothing: Wear loose-fitting, breathable clothing made from natural fibers like cotton to prevent friction and irritation.
  • Keep a Symptom Diary: Record when hives occur and what you were doing or eating before they appeared. This can help identify triggers.
  • Stress Management: Stress can sometimes exacerbate hives. Practice stress-reduction techniques such as deep breathing, yoga, or meditation.
  • Avoid Alcohol and Certain Foods: Some people with hives find that alcohol, certain foods (like spicy foods and high histamine foods), and caffeine can worsen symptoms. Consider avoiding these triggers.
  • Consult an Allergist: If hives are recurrent or chronic, consulting an allergist can help identify specific triggers through allergy testing.
